## Executive Summary

The 2026 ASU+GSV Summit made one thing clear: the debate has shifted from "will AI transform education" to "how fast, and who gets left behind."

**Five signals stood out:**

1. **AI tutoring is crossing from novelty to necessity.** Reed Hastings argued that the classroom's "sage on the stage" model is the steam engine's power distribution system, and AI tutoring is the individual electric motor. Connor Zwick (Speak) reported 3-5x faster language acquisition with AI tutors. David Rogier (MasterClass) said an education equivalent to a 2-year MBA can now be delivered for $100. The consensus: individualized AI tutoring will become the default within 5 years, not as a replacement for human connection, but as a replacement for mass lecture.

2. **Assessment is the next crisis.** Multiple sessions surfaced the same problem: if students use AI to write, written assignments stop being reliable signals of understanding. Connor Zwick predicted "testing as a concept will feel extremely antiquated" within 5 years, replaced by continuous mastery measurement. A growing number of companies are building conversational assessment (oral exams via AI) as an alternative.

3. **Career navigation is being rebuilt from scratch.** The BrightBound panel revealed that families now pay $50,000 for career coaching, just as they did for college admissions. Julia Dixon (ESAI) is building "narrative intelligence" to help students tell their unique stories. Nick Gross (Find Your Grind) showed a 20% increase in student engagement with an ESSA Tier 2 study. Keely Cat-Wells (Making Space) is closing the disability employment gap, reporting that self-identification of disability doubled after a single employer engagement.

4. **The speed of AI is outrunning institutions.** Austin from Gauntlet AI described completely rewriting curriculum every 10 weeks because AI changes that fast. Alpha School compresses a full day of academics into 2 hours of AI-assisted mastery learning, freeing afternoons for project-based life skills. Multiple speakers noted that traditional higher ed can't change curriculum in less than 3 years.

5. **Bipartisan consensus on education is fragile but real.** Former Secretaries of Education Margaret Spellings (R) and Arne Duncan (D) appeared together. Rahm Emanuel called for an "education reset" tied to outcomes. State chiefs from Rhode Island, Virginia, and DC discussed system-level transformation. The civic education sessions showed real concern about democratic participation.

## Theme 1: AI Tutoring and Personalized Learning

**Key speakers:** Reed Hastings (Anthropic/Netflix), Adeel Khan (MagicSchool AI), Connor Zwick (Speak), David Rogier (MasterClass), Mackenzie Price (Alpha School), Po-Shen Loh (Carnegie Mellon)

### The Argument

The strongest case for AI tutoring came from Reed Hastings, speaking from the board of Anthropic and 25 years of education work. His analogy: factories didn't get more productive when they replaced the steam engine with an electric engine. Productivity only jumped when they gave each machine its own motor. Education's "sage on the stage" is the power distribution system. AI tutoring is the individual motor.

Hastings is funding a year-long experiment: 50 random kids from a median school get full-time individual human tutors for a year, to establish what the actual rate of learning is when instruction is fully individualized. He suspects it's 2x the current rate, meaning students could reach college-level knowledge by 11th grade.

Alpha School is already running a version of this. Their model: 2 hours of AI-assisted mastery-based learning in the morning, project-based life skills in the afternoon. Students in the room next to each other work at completely different levels. A 7-year-old does algebra while the peer next to them works on basic math facts.

Connor Zwick (Speak) reported that AI-powered language tutoring delivers fluency 3-5x faster than traditional methods, because the AI has "perfect memory of everything you've ever said into the app" and adjusts lessons accordingly.

David Rogier (MasterClass) was blunt: "If you aren't in the top 10 MBA programs in the country, you aren't going to exist in 5-10 years." MasterClass launched a 12-week program with University of Chicago Booth and OpenAI. For 500 slots, over 12,000 applied.

### The Counterpoint

Adeel Khan (MagicSchool AI) pushed back on the idea that AI alone is sufficient. His argument: "the alchemy of wisdom and relationship creates incredible learning." MagicSchool's approach is to amplify teachers, not replace them. They give districts a toggle to turn off student-facing AI features, and many districts use it.

### What This Means for Institutions

The question is no longer whether AI tutoring works. It's whether your institution is ready for students who arrive having learned 2-3x faster than your curriculum assumes. The early movers (Alpha School, Speak, Gauntlet) are building the evidence base. Institutions that wait for the evidence to be conclusive will find the evidence was built by their competitors.

## Theme 2: The Assessment Crisis

**Key speakers:** Connor Zwick (Speak), Nick Gross (Find Your Grind), Mackenzie Price (Alpha School), Austin Allred (Gauntlet AI)

### The Problem

If AI can write essays, take tests, and generate code, how do you know what a student actually knows? This question surfaced in nearly every education-focused session.

Connor Zwick's prediction was the sharpest: "Testing as a concept will feel extremely antiquated in 5 years." Speak is building continuous mastery measurement that tracks exactly what you know and don't know, replacing periodic testing with ongoing skill verification.

### Emerging Solutions

- **Conversational assessment:** AI-powered oral exams and role-play scenarios are emerging as alternatives to written work. If essays are unreliable, live conversation becomes a stronger signal of understanding.
- **Continuous mastery tracking:** Speak and Alpha School both use AI to track student mastery in real-time, adjusting instruction accordingly. No separate "test day" needed.
- **Portfolio-based evidence:** Nick Gross (Find Your Grind) gives students AI-generated "About Me" portfolios that synthesize everything they've explored, shifting assessment from "did you pass the test" to "what have you demonstrated over time."

### What This Means for Institutions

Every institution that relies on written assignments as a primary assessment method needs a plan. The companies building alternatives are still early, but the problem is immediate.

## Theme 3: Career Navigation and Workforce Readiness

**Key speakers:** Julia Dixon (ESAI), Nick Gross (Find Your Grind), Keely Cat-Wells (Making Space), Sam Hyams (SpringPod), Rowan Trollope (BrightBound), Joseph Fuller (Harvard Business School)

### The Landscape

The career navigation panel at ASU+GSV revealed a market in transition. The old model: take a career assessment, get 3 careers spit out, good luck. The new model: continuous self-discovery, AI-powered matching, and portfolio-based evidence of skills.

Julia Dixon (ESAI) coined "narrative intelligence": the ability to find language for your values, motivations, and interests, and connect them to opportunities. Her product sources opportunities "in your sleep" but emphasizes that volume without self-knowledge is just "scaling sameness."

Sam Hyams (SpringPod) showed concrete numbers: Airbus went from 100 students on in-person work experience to 10,000 students via virtual experiences. A third of students in the Amazon program end up in tech-aligned roles, tracked via LinkedIn.

Keely Cat-Wells (Making Space) brought the disability lens: 40% of employed disabled people are underemployed. Her platform helps disabled professionals reframe lived experience as transferable skills and helps employers create accessible workspaces. After a single Making Space engagement, employer disability self-identification rates doubled.

### Key Tensions

- **Volume vs. depth:** AI makes it trivial to apply to 100 jobs. But does that help anyone? Dixon's argument: standing out requires human storytelling, not mass applications.
- **Employer pull vs. student push:** SpringPod solves the demand side (employers creating virtual experiences). Find Your Grind solves the supply side (student self-discovery). The market needs both.
- **Measurement gap:** Sam Hyams made the sharpest point: "Measurement is the bit that's always been missing from the career navigation world." Most tools measure engagement or satisfaction, not whether the student actually got a better job.

## Theme 4: EdTech Business and Investment

**Key speakers:** David Rogier (MasterClass), Austin Allred (Gauntlet AI), Michael Moe (GSV), Reed Hastings (Anthropic/Netflix), Sam Hyams (SpringPod), Nick Gross (Find Your Grind)

### Market Signals

David Rogier's claim that MBAs outside the top 10 won't exist in 5-10 years is extreme but directional. The MasterClass/Booth/OpenAI 12-week program with 12,000 applicants for 500 slots demonstrates massive demand for compressed, high-quality credentialing.

Austin from Gauntlet AI described a model where the company pays for everything (food, housing, travel) and employers pay upfront to access graduates. Some graduates command salaries near $1 million. The model works because AI engineering talent doesn't exist in traditional pipelines: "It has to be created for the employers."

### Advice for Founders

Reed Hastings' advice to EdTech entrepreneurs was split: "If you want to make money, sell to school districts, make teachers' lives easier. If you want to change the world, focus on homeschoolers. Focus on people who are able to go at their own pace." Build the self-driving car of education, not the map in the car.

Sam Hyams offered practical guidance: "There is no excuse not to have a working prototype now. With vibe coding, you should be able to spin that up quickly." Go straight to the user first, even if the buyer is the district.

Nick Gross on what he looks for when investing: founders who can define outcomes, have validated market need (not assumed it), and understand that EdTech sales cycles are brutal.

## Theme 5: Education Policy and Reform

**Key speakers:** Margaret Spellings (former U.S. Secretary of Education), Arne Duncan (former U.S. Secretary of Education), Rahm Emanuel (former Mayor of Chicago), Geoffrey Canada (Harlem Children's Zone), Greg Lukianoff (FIRE), Governor Eric Holcomb (Indiana), Julia Minson (Harvard)

### Bipartisan Moments

Margaret Spellings (Bush administration) and Arne Duncan (Obama administration), two former Secretaries of Education, appeared together at ASU+GSV. Their session title says it: "Can't We Be Friends... It's Not People, It's Parties."

Rahm Emanuel called for an "education reset" focused on fundamentals, accountability, and tying funding to outcomes. His record in Chicago: universal pre-K, expanded instructional time, free community college.

State education chiefs from Rhode Island (Angélica Infante-Green), Virginia (Aimee Guidera), DC (Dr. Christina Grant), and others reflected on leading complex systems through change. The recurring theme: leaders who drive improvement often see their work dismantled by the next administration. Reed Hastings called this the "hero syndrome."

### Free Speech and Civic Education

Greg Lukianoff (FIRE) examined cancel culture, censorship, and open debate on campus. State leaders from New York, Indiana, and elsewhere discussed the future of civics education. Governor Eric Holcomb (Indiana) and Julia Minson (Harvard) explored leadership, civility, and disagreement.

## Theme 6: Equity, Access, and Global Perspectives

**Key speakers:** Reed Hastings (Anthropic/Netflix), Michael Crow (ASU President), will.i.am (FYI.AI), Goldie Hawn (MindUP Foundation), Lisa Gevelber (Grow with Google), Sonya Christian (CA Community Colleges), Philip Colligan (Raspberry Pi Foundation)

### The Global AI Access Story

Reed Hastings described a project in Rwanda: one tablet per child, with the goal of making Rwanda "the most successful education state, first in Africa, maybe in the world." His argument: when we figure out software-based AI teaching, sharing it costs almost nothing. A phone in Kibera runs the same OS as a phone in San Francisco.

Michael Crow (ASU President) and will.i.am discussed AI as "pro-human" in education and work, alongside Sonya Christian (CA Community Colleges Chancellor).

Lisa Gevelber (Grow with Google) explored network effects of AI in higher education, building on Google Career Certificates that have helped over 1 million people upskill.

Paula Golden (Broadcom Foundation) and Philip Colligan (Raspberry Pi Foundation) focused on computing and AI literacy as an access mechanism for STEM education.

## What I'm Watching

Based on what I saw at the summit and my meetings:

1. **AI tutoring consolidation.** The space is crowded with companies offering Socratic-method tutoring with similar interfaces and similar claims. The differentiator won't be the tutoring itself. It'll be integration depth with existing LMS infrastructure, subject specificity, and evidence of outcomes.

2. **Assessment as the next big category.** Conversational assessment (AI-powered oral exams and role-play scenarios) is emerging as a direct response to the "if AI can write, how do you test?" problem. Early, but the need is urgent and underserved. Institutions need alternatives to written assignments now.

3. **Retention going autonomous.** The student retention space is shifting from alert-based systems (flagging at-risk students for human follow-up) toward autonomous action, where AI agents initiate interventions directly. That's the architectural shift this category has needed.

4. **The instructional design tools maturing.** AI-powered platforms for instructional designers are solving real workflow problems for faculty: course scaffolding, content migration across LMS platforms, FERPA-compliant deployment. This is the "make teachers' lives easier" category Hastings identified.
