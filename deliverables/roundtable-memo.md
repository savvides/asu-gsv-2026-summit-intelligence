# What If Universities Stopped Running the Same Pilot Twice?

**Roundtable Synthesis -- ASU+GSV Summit 2026**
April 15, 2026 | Presented by Philippos Savvides, ASU ScaleU

---

Thank you to the 15 of you who stayed, pushed back, and shared real stories in this session. This memo is my honest attempt to capture what was said -- not to sell anything, but to give everyone in the room a shared artifact we can build on.

What follows is organized by theme, not by chronology. Quotes are lightly edited for clarity and anonymized by role.

---

## 1. The Sourcing Problem

One of the clearest pain points in the room had nothing to do with pilots themselves. It was about what happens before a pilot even begins: finding the right companies to test.

A VP of Online Learning at a public university described the reality bluntly: she doesn't have time for a full scan of the market. Her procurement threshold is $50K, her staff are stretched thin, and she rarely feels confident that the RFP mix includes the full slate of potential vendors. Her word for it was "a gamble."

This isn't a failure of diligence. It's a structural constraint. University teams are small. EdTech markets are fragmented. And when you're running a department, sourcing vendors is never your only job -- it's the thing you do between your actual jobs.

An Innovation Director at a European business school offered a complementary perspective. He said he'd love to see how other institutions pilot technology -- not just what they chose, but how they evaluated it. If two or three peer institutions had tested a product successfully, that wouldn't just inform his decision. It would give him ammunition to convince internal stakeholders to take the risk.

The ask from institutions wasn't "tell me what to buy." It was "help me see what's out there and who's already tested it."

---

## 2. The Pilot Paradox

When institutions do find a company worth testing, the next challenge is figuring out how to test it without triggering a 12-month procurement cycle.

Several administrators in the room described running what they called "dirty pilots" -- minimum viable tests designed to generate signal before committing to full integration. One administrator was candid: "We try not to involve the IT department, maybe, because they will come with such a long list... let's make that dirty test, really. And then if it's good enough, I'll take you to the CIO."

This isn't recklessness. It's pragmatism. The VP of Online Learning at a public university described a more structured version of the same instinct. Her process depends on the product: which systems it touches, whether it requires the registrar's office, OIT, or instructional design teams. She uses lightweight pilots to see if anyone actually uses the product before investing in a full integration review.

The paradox is this: the formal process exists for good reason (security, compliance, interoperability), but it's often too slow and too heavy for early-stage evaluation. So institutions build informal workarounds -- which work, but don't scale, don't transfer, and leave no documentation behind for the next team that faces the same decision.

---

## 3. The Time Constraint

From the founder side of the table, the most consistent frustration was time -- not money, not skepticism, but the sheer difficulty of getting institutional partners to carve out bandwidth for a pilot.

A founder building identity profiles for students and career counseling said time is the number-one blocker, even when the institution is genuinely excited about the product. Even when the time requirement from the institution's side is low, it becomes a roadblock.

My response in the session was direct, and I want to be honest about it here: if an institution doesn't have time for your pilot, it usually means the problem you're solving isn't painful enough for them right now. That's not a judgment of your product. It's a signal about customer fit. The right institution will find the time because the pain is real enough to prioritize.

A representative from a math and AI tutoring platform reframed the time problem from a different angle: "If you could do it today, or in two weeks, do some testing, and get some results and feedback -- that's better than $50,000 probability of $50,000 in six months." Speed of learning, in other words, often matters more than scale of investment.

---

## 4. The Trust Asymmetry

This was the core tension I presented, and the room confirmed it.

Companies that pilot at ASU often run nearly identical pilots at the next institution. The evidence from Pilot A is collected carefully -- outcomes, usage data, testimonials. But that evidence is almost never accepted at face value by Institution B. Institutions discount each other's pilot outcomes. Different student populations, different contexts, different infrastructure. Fair enough.

But here's the asymmetry: institutions will trust a peer's protocol much more readily than a peer's results. They won't adopt your verdict, but they'll adopt your process. They won't take your word that a product works, but they'll take your framework for figuring out whether it works.

This distinction -- trusting the filter, not the verdict -- was the most resonant idea in the room.

---

## 5. The Proposition

Based on the trust asymmetry, the proposition I put forward was deliberately narrow.

What if institutions shared the filter, not the verdict? Specifically:

- **Share the protocol** for evaluating whether a company is pilot-ready -- security, accessibility, stage of development, problem-solution fit.
- **Share the shortlist** of companies that have cleared the protocol -- not as endorsements, but as a pre-vetted starting point.
- **Share the playbook** for running a pilot in 12 weeks instead of 12 months -- the actual steps, timelines, and stakeholder choreography.

What would NOT be shared: outcome data, procurement decisions, or vendor endorsements. No institution would be asked to vouch for a product. The shared layer would be the evaluation process, not the evaluation outcome.

This matters because it respects the reality that every institution has unique context -- while acknowledging that the process of figuring out "is this company worth our time?" is surprisingly similar across institutions.

---

## 6. Where This Breaks

The room was honest about the limits, and I want to be equally honest here.

**Internal context still matters.** The Innovation Director at the European business school pointed out that even when a technology is proven elsewhere, running it at his institution requires what he called "program context" -- a willing department, a specific program, an internal champion who will absorb the risk. No shared protocol solves that.

**Procurement thresholds vary wildly.** Arizona's threshold is $100K. Ohio's is $50K. Other institutions in the room had different numbers and different state-specific RFP requirements. A shared playbook has to account for this variation or it becomes irrelevant.

**The RFP black box.** A founder raised a point that resonated with several others: RFP processes give no feedback to non-winners. Companies invest significant time responding to RFPs and receive nothing back -- no explanation, no scoring, no indication of what would have made the difference. This opacity makes it harder for companies to improve and harder for the market to get smarter over time.

---

## 7. What Works at ASU

For context, here is what the process looks like on our side.

ASU's ScaleU team runs a needs assessment across 17 colleges, then uses a structured intake form for potential partners covering security, accessibility, stage of development, and the specific problem being solved. A team of five evaluates companies before anyone else at the university sees them. In four years, we've run more than 25 pilots.

We actively seek out early-stage companies -- not because they're cheap, but because they're willing to co-develop and be real partners. They'll adjust the product. They'll sit in the room. They'll iterate.

We also have tough conversations early: What's your pilot cost? What's your post-pilot cost? Do you have a pricing structure that's scaled so we can grow into it? These questions aren't adversarial. They're how we figure out whether a partnership has legs.

This process is not perfect, and it was built for ASU's specific context. But much of the underlying logic -- the intake criteria, the evaluation sequence, the stakeholder choreography -- is transferable. That's what I'm trying to make available.

I'm building a public playbook for EdTech pilots in higher education. It will include evaluation criteria, pilot readiness frameworks, and what I learn from ongoing conversations with institutions and companies. The link is coming soon.
