# What If Universities Stopped Running the Same Pilot Twice?

**Roundtable Synthesis -- ASU+GSV Summit 2026**
April 15, 2026 | Presented by Philippos Savvides, ASU ScaleU

---

This was the last session of the summit. Small room, end of day four. The fact that people were still showing up, still engaged, and stayed after to keep talking tells me this topic hit a nerve. What follows is my attempt to capture what the room actually said. Not to sell anything. Just to give everyone a shared artifact we can build on.

Organized by theme, not chronology. Quotes are lightly edited for clarity and anonymized by role.

---

## 1. Before a pilot even starts

The clearest pain point in the room had nothing to do with pilots themselves. It was about what happens before: finding the right companies to test.

A VP of Online Learning at a public university put it simply. She doesn't have time for a full scan of the market. Her procurement threshold is $50K, her staff are stretched, and she rarely feels confident that the RFP mix includes the full slate of potential vendors. Her word for it was "a gamble."

And that's not a failure on her part. University teams are small. EdTech markets are fragmented. When you're running a department, sourcing vendors is the thing you do between your actual jobs.

An Innovation Director at a European business school reframed it. He said he'd love to see how other institutions pilot technology, not just what they chose, but how they evaluated it. If two or three peer institutions had tested a product successfully, that wouldn't just inform his decision. It would give him ammunition to convince internal stakeholders to take the risk.

The ask wasn't "tell me what to buy." It was "help me see what's out there and who's already tested it."

---

## 2. Dirty pilots

When institutions do find a company worth testing, the next challenge is figuring out how to test it without triggering a 12-month procurement cycle.

Several administrators described running what they called "dirty pilots," minimum viable tests designed to generate signal before committing to full integration. One was candid about it: "We try not to involve the IT department, maybe, because they will come with such a long list... let's make that dirty test, really. And then if it's good enough, I'll take you to the CIO."

I heard this from more than one person. The VP of Online Learning described a more structured version of the same instinct. Her process depends on the product: which systems it touches, whether it requires the registrar's office, OIT, or instructional design teams. She uses lightweight pilots to see if anyone actually uses the product before investing in a full integration review.

The formal process exists for good reason (security, compliance, interoperability). But it's often too slow for early-stage evaluation. So institutions build informal workarounds. These work, but they don't scale, they don't transfer, and they leave no documentation behind for the next team facing the same decision.

---

## 3. The real blocker is bandwidth

From the founder side of the table, the most consistent frustration was time. Not money, not skepticism. Just the sheer difficulty of getting institutional partners to carve out bandwidth.

A founder building identity profiles for students said time is the number-one blocker, even when the institution is genuinely excited about the product.

I'll be honest about what I said in the session: if an institution doesn't have time for your pilot, it usually means the problem you're solving isn't painful enough for them right now. That's not a judgment of your product. It's a signal about customer fit.

A representative from a math and AI tutoring platform put it differently: "If you could do it today, or in two weeks, do some testing, and get some results and feedback, that's better than $50,000 probability of $50,000 in six months." Speed of learning matters more than scale of investment.

---

## 4. Trust the process, not the verdict

This was the core tension I presented, and the room confirmed it.

Companies that pilot at ASU often run nearly identical pilots at the next institution. The evidence from Pilot A is collected carefully, outcomes, usage data, testimonials. But that evidence is almost never accepted at face value by Institution B. Different student populations, different contexts, different infrastructure. Fair enough.

But here's what I keep seeing: institutions will trust a peer's protocol much more readily than a peer's results. They won't adopt your verdict, but they'll adopt your process. They won't take your word that a product works, but they'll take your framework for figuring out whether it works.

That distinction, trusting the filter not the verdict, is where everyone in the room seemed to nod.

---

## 5. What I proposed

Based on that, the proposition I put forward was deliberately narrow.

What if institutions shared the filter, not the verdict? Specifically:

- **Share the protocol** for evaluating whether a company is pilot-ready, covering security, accessibility, stage of development, problem-solution fit.
- **Share the shortlist** of companies that have cleared the protocol, not as endorsements, but as a pre-vetted starting point.
- **Share the playbook** for running a pilot in 12 weeks instead of 12 months, the actual steps, timelines, and stakeholder choreography.

What would NOT be shared: outcome data, procurement decisions, or vendor endorsements. No institution would be asked to vouch for a product. The shared layer is the evaluation process, not the evaluation outcome.

This works because every institution has unique context, but the process of figuring out "is this company worth our time?" is surprisingly similar across institutions.

---

## 6. Where this falls apart

The room was honest about the limits.

**Internal context still matters.** The Innovation Director at the European business school pointed out that even when a technology is proven elsewhere, running it at his institution requires what he called "program context," a willing department, a specific program, an internal champion who will absorb the risk. No shared protocol solves that.

**Procurement thresholds vary wildly.** Arizona's is $100K. Ohio's is $50K. Other institutions had different numbers and different state-specific RFP requirements. A shared playbook has to account for this variation or it becomes irrelevant.

**The RFP black box.** A founder raised something that resonated with several others: RFP processes give no feedback to non-winners. Companies invest significant time responding and receive nothing back, no explanation, no scoring, no indication of what would have made the difference. That opacity makes it harder for companies to improve and harder for the market to get smarter over time.

---

## 7. What works at ASU

For context, here's what the process looks like on our side.

ScaleU runs a needs assessment across 17 colleges, then uses a structured intake form covering security, accessibility, stage of development, and the specific problem being solved. A team of five evaluates companies before anyone else at the university sees them. We've built a track record of running pilots.

We actively seek out early-stage companies, not because they're cheap, but because they're willing to co-develop and be real partners. They'll adjust the product. They'll sit in the room. They'll iterate.

We also have tough conversations early. What's your pilot cost? What's your post-pilot cost? Do you have a pricing structure that scales so we can grow into it? These aren't adversarial questions. They're how we figure out whether a partnership has legs.

This process is not perfect, and it was built for ASU's specific context. But much of the underlying logic, the intake criteria, the evaluation sequence, the stakeholder choreography, is transferable. That's what I'm trying to make available.

---

## What's next

I'm working on a public playbook for EdTech pilots in higher education. Evaluation criteria, pilot readiness frameworks, and what I keep learning from conversations with institutions and companies.

If the ideas in this memo hold up, the playbook should make it easier for any institution to run a structured pilot, and easier for any company to know what "pilot-ready" actually means.
