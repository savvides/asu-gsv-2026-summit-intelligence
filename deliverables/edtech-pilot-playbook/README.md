# EdTech Pilot Playbook

A practical framework for running structured EdTech pilots in higher education. For institutions evaluating technology and for founders building it.

Built from 4,000+ company reviews, 30+ paid pilots, and conversations with institutional leaders across the US and Europe. Published under CC BY 4.0 because methodology should be shared, not hoarded.

## The Problem This Solves

Universities evaluate the same EdTech companies independently. Each institution runs its own discovery, its own diligence, its own pilot design. A company that validates at one university runs a nearly identical pilot at the next. Evidence from Pilot A is collected but almost never accepted by Institution B.

The cost is redundancy. For institutions, it's staff time spent re-evaluating products that peers have already tested. For founders, it's 12-18 month sales cycles repeated at every institution.

This playbook shares the **filter**, not the verdict. The evaluation methodology, the pilot design framework, and the readiness criteria are public. What each institution decides to buy is still theirs.

### What We Heard From Institutional Leaders

At a recent roundtable with university administrators and EdTech founders, several patterns emerged:

**The sourcing gap.** A VP of Online Learning at a public university: "I don't have time for a full scan of the market. I have a list of things I'm looking to accomplish, and I'm on the hunt for solutions. What I don't have time for is to evaluate everything. The RFP process is a gamble because I don't see the full vendor slate."

**The peer validation signal.** An Innovation Director at a European business school: "If two or three other universities had tested this successfully, that would help me convince my internal stakeholders to take the risk."

**The time constraint.** Multiple institutional leaders said their staff are stretched thin. Budget isn't always the barrier. Time to evaluate, integrate, and run a pilot is the real bottleneck, even for products with low institutional requirements.

**The "dirty pilot."** Several administrators described running lightweight, minimal tests before involving IT or procurement. One said: "Let's not worry about 100% of the tech integration. Let's see if it works. Let's see what our students think. If nobody uses it, nobody wants to use it, we're not going to keep going."

**The founder perspective.** Founders described relationship-based selling as the only reliable approach at early stage. RFP processes were described as one-sided: non-winners get no feedback, and the cost of preparing a proposal is high relative to the odds. One founder from a math/AI tutoring company said: "If you could do it today, or in two weeks, get some results and feedback, that's better than the probability of $50,000 in six months."

## Pilot Readiness Checklist

Before you evaluate a single product, answer these questions. They separate institutions that are ready to pilot from those that are still figuring out what they need.

### Part 1: Needs Discovery

Most institutions get pitched by companies before they've defined what they're looking for. The result is vendor-driven evaluation: you react to what's offered rather than seeking what you need.

Start here instead:

**1. What specific gap in the student journey are you trying to close?**

Map your student lifecycle: Pre-Enroll, Apply, Onboard, Select & Enroll, Course Experience, Graduate & Beyond. Which phase has the most friction? Where are students dropping off, struggling, or underserved?

If you can't name a specific phase and a specific problem, you're not ready to evaluate products. You're ready to run a needs assessment.

**2. What does "better" look like, and how would you measure it?**

Define the outcome before you see the product. Complete this sentence: "[Target population] who use [intervention] will show [measurable outcome] compared to [comparison group], as measured by [metric] over [time period]."

If you can't complete this sentence, the pilot won't produce evidence anyone can act on.

**3. Who currently owns this problem?**

Name the person. Not the department. The person who wakes up thinking about this gap, who would be the internal champion for a pilot, and who controls (or can request) the budget.

If the answer is "nobody specifically," the pilot will die from lack of ownership, regardless of product quality.

**4. What are you doing about this problem right now?**

Even if the answer is "nothing formal," there's always a workaround: spreadsheets, manual processes, adjunct labor, duct-taped tools. Name the workaround and estimate what it costs in time, money, or outcomes.

If the current state is genuinely "nothing" and no one is bothered, the problem isn't painful enough to sustain a pilot.

### Part 2: Readiness Assessment

Once you know what you need, assess whether your institution can actually run a structured pilot:

| Question | Ready | Not Yet |
|---|---|---|
| Do you have a named budget holder for EdTech pilots? | A specific person can authorize pilot spending | "We'd need to find budget" |
| What's your procurement threshold before RFP is required? | You know the dollar amount (e.g., $50K, $100K) | You're not sure |
| Do you have a team that can run a 12-week pilot? | Instructional designers, IT support, faculty champions identified | "We'd need to assemble a team" |
| Can you provide a cohort of 200+ students? | A specific course, program, or population identified | "We'd need to find willing faculty" |
| Does your institution have a security review process (FERPA, SOC 2, HECCVAT)? | Process exists, timeline is known | No formal process or timeline unknown |

**Scoring:** If you answered "Ready" to 4-5 questions, you can run a structured pilot. 2-3, you can run a lightweight "dirty pilot" to test demand before investing in full process. 0-1, focus on the needs discovery first.

## Evaluation Criteria

This is the filter we use to assess whether an EdTech company is ready for a structured institutional pilot. It's designed for seed-stage companies ($500K-$3M raised) with a functional product.

Institutions can adopt or adapt these criteria. Founders can use them to self-assess before approaching institutions.

### Hard Filters (Pass/Fail)

Before scoring, every company must clear these binary gates:

1. **Institutional buyer exists.** Can you name a specific person at a specific institution who would champion this product? "Higher education" is not a buyer. A Dean of Online Learning at a specific university is.
2. **The problem is real.** Is there evidence (not assumption) that the stated problem exists and matters to institutional stakeholders?
3. **A product exists.** Not a pitch deck. Not a prototype. A product that users can actually use today.
4. **Validation is possible.** Can you design a pilot that produces measurable evidence within 12-18 months?
5. **The founder will engage.** Is the founder willing to treat the pilot as an evidence-generation instrument, not a sales demo?

If any answer is "no," the company is not ready for a structured pilot, regardless of product quality.

### Eight-Dimension Scoring Rubric

Each dimension is scored 1-5. Minimum threshold to advance: 24 out of 40.

| Dimension | What It Measures | Score 1 (Weak) | Score 5 (Strong) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Problem Importance** | Does this solve a problem institutions actively struggle with? | Nice-to-have, no urgency | Critical gap, budget already allocated |
| **Founder-Market Fit** | Does the founder deeply understand the institutional context? | First-time in education, learning on the job | Years of direct experience with the buyer persona |
| **Founder Quality** | Can they execute with intellectual honesty? | Defensive about feedback, treats pilot as sales | Treats pilot as research, adapts based on evidence |
| **Solution Effectiveness** | Does the product actually work? | Demo-stage, limited evidence | Deployed, measurable outcomes, user retention data |
| **Market Scale** | How large is the addressable market? | Single institution, single use case | Multi-institution, multi-department applicability |
| **Collaboration Potential** | Will this company be a genuine partner? | Transactional, seeking logo | Co-development mindset, responsive to institutional needs |
| **Ethical Commitment** | Does the product handle data, access, and equity responsibly? | No privacy policy, no accessibility plan | FERPA-ready, WCAG-compliant, transparent data practices |
| **Technical Innovation** | Is the technology defensible? | Commodity feature, easily replicated | Proprietary approach, durable competitive advantage |

### The Moat Durability Test

One additional lens for AI-era evaluation: **Does this product survive an LLM plus a two-week prompt engineering sprint by a competent IT team?**

Pure software is increasingly exposed to AI substitution. The question is not whether a product uses AI, but whether its core value survives replication. Four durable moats apply:

1. **Proprietary data networks.** Value increases with usage data that competitors can't replicate.
2. **Deep institutional integration.** Embedded in workflows (LMS, SIS, SSO) in ways that create switching costs.
3. **Supply-side network effects.** Each new user or institution makes the product more valuable for all others.
4. **Regulated access.** Certifications, compliance requirements, or licensed content that create barriers to entry.

"AI-powered" is not a moat. It's a feature layer exposed to the same substitution risk it claims to solve.

## How to Run a Pilot in 12 Weeks

A structured pilot that produces actionable evidence, not just "we tried it and it was fine."

### Week 1-2: Design

1. **State the hypothesis.** Use the template: "[Target population] who use [product] will show [measurable outcome] compared to [control], as measured by [metric] over [time period]."
2. **Identify the cohort.** Minimum 200 students for statistical significance. Name the course(s), the faculty champion(s), and the IT contact.
3. **Define success criteria.** What outcome means "go" (proceed to procurement)? What means "pivot" (adjust and re-test)? What means "no-go" (end the relationship)?
4. **Negotiate terms.** Pilot cost, post-pilot pricing, data ownership, support commitments. Have these conversations before deployment, not after.

### Week 3-4: Setup

5. **Security review.** FERPA, SOC 2, HECCVAT, LTI 1.3 as applicable. This is where timelines stretch. Start early.
6. **Technical integration.** LMS integration, SSO, data feeds. For a "dirty pilot," skip full integration: use standalone access with manual enrollment.
7. **Faculty briefing.** The faculty champion needs to understand the hypothesis, their role, and what data will be collected.

### Week 5-10: Execution

8. **Launch with the cohort.** Track adoption (who's using it), engagement (how much), and the primary outcome metric.
9. **Weekly check-ins with the company.** Are there issues? What are they seeing in the data? This is co-development, not vendor management.
10. **Mid-pilot review (week 7-8).** Is the hypothesis testable given actual adoption? If adoption is too low, diagnose why before the pilot ends.

### Week 11-12: Evidence

11. **Analyze results.** Statistical significance on the primary outcome? Qualitative feedback from students and faculty?
12. **Decision gate.** Go (budget holder commits to procurement), Pivot (adjust scope or population and re-test), or No-Go (end with documented reasons).
13. **Debrief with the company.** Share the evidence, including negative findings. This is what distinguishes a validation partner from a customer.

## What Institutions Actually Care About

For founders: here's what matters in the evaluation, ranked by what actually drives decisions.

**Tier 1: Deal-breakers (must have)**
- Security compliance (FERPA, SOC 2, accessibility)
- A named institutional champion who will own the pilot internally
- Pricing that works for a pilot (not enterprise-scale from day one)

**Tier 2: Differentiators (separate winners from the pack)**
- Evidence from prior deployments (even small-scale)
- Willingness to co-develop based on pilot feedback
- Integration depth with existing systems (Canvas, LMS workflows)

**Tier 3: Signals (things evaluators notice)**
- Founder's depth of understanding of institutional context
- How the founder handles the question "what happens if the pilot doesn't work?"
- Whether the founder treats the pilot as research or as a sales opportunity

**What does NOT matter as much as founders think:**
- The technology itself (institutions buy outcomes, not architecture)
- Awards and press coverage
- "AI-powered" as a differentiator (every product claims this now)
