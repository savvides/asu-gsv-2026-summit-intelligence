# Why Education Leaders Are Switching

**Demand Signals from Five ASU+GSV 2026 Summit Panels**

Philippos Savvides, Head of ScaleU | April 2026

---

The [Summit Intelligence Report](../synthesis/summit-intelligence-report.md) covers what happened at ASU+GSV 2026. What speakers presented, which themes dominated, what the consensus looked like across 461 sessions.

This document asks a different question: why are education leaders actually changing what they do?

I went back to five panel transcripts from the summit and analyzed the switching behavior underneath the talking points. Not what people said they believe, but what they described doing differently, what pushed them to move, what pulled them toward a new approach, and what almost stopped them. The five panels covered career navigation at scale (Brent Haken, Oklahoma DCTE), corporate workforce development (Josh, Walmart Academy), AI-powered tutoring for kids (Sam Lam, ClassDojo), virtual career experiences (Sam Hyams, SpringPod), and the big picture on education and AI (Reed Hastings).

A note on evidence quality: these are conference panels, not structured research interviews. People are presenting, not confessing. Three of the five are vendors or institutional leaders describing their customers' switches, not their own. The patterns here are directional. They're worth acting on, but they should be validated with follow-up conversations before making high-stakes decisions.

---

## Three jobs that keep coming up

Across five very different panels, three recurring needs kept surfacing. Not features. Not product categories. Jobs that decision-makers are trying to get done.

### 1. "Help me reach more people without hiring proportionally more staff"

This showed up in three of five panels, and the stories were strikingly similar in structure even though the contexts were completely different.

Sam Hyams described how Airbus used to cap their work experience program at about a hundred students per year. That was the physical limit of what they could run in person. Through SpringPod's virtual experiences, they now serve 10,000 students a year. Same employer engagement, 100x the reach.

Brent Haken told a version of the same story from the public sector. Oklahoma has 500+ school districts. For years, the state mandated personalized learning plans and assumed technology platforms would make them happen. They didn't. "We were checking the box," Brent said. Schools would walk students through the plans on paper, mark them complete, and move on. The breakthrough wasn't a better platform. It was deploying a field team where each person owns a geography, visits every school in person, and builds relationships with counselors. Technology plus people, not technology instead of people.

Reed Hastings framed it at the largest scale. He's spent 25 years working on education through policy, charters, and EdTech. His verdict on each phase was blunt. Policy moved slowly. Charters produced real results but "it's grindingly slow. Think trench warfare." EdTech so far hasn't produced discernible change in learning outcomes. His current bet is AI-powered individual tutoring, precisely because software scales in a way that none of the previous approaches could. "When we figure out software-based AI teaching that really does all the work, we can share that with the entire world. A phone in Kibera runs basically the same operating system that we run."

The pattern: every one of these leaders hit a structural ceiling. Not "this could be better" but "this cannot grow." The switch happens when someone finds a model that breaks through the ceiling without proportional cost increases.

### 2. "Stop wasting people's time on mechanical tasks so they can do the human work"

Also three of five panels. The framing was remarkably consistent: time and attention are being consumed by low-skill, performative, or mechanical work that actively prevents people from doing what they were hired to do.

Josh from Walmart Academy described a task called VisPick. Associates used to spend about an hour scanning barcodes to locate items in the backroom. Computer vision reduced that to twenty minutes. But the real point wasn't the time savings. "What happens is you get the time back to, you know, for managers, you're leading your teams, for associates, you're helping customers. It's giving time back for more human activities than the manual activities." The technology didn't replace anyone. It returned time to the human parts of the job.

Sam Lam at ClassDojo was direct about the failure mode of the first wave. "I think we all probably saw, a year ago, kind of the crap version of this, which was like, here's a chatbot. Please talk to the chatbot and tell it to do things for you. This is like, it's fine, but it doesn't change anyone's life." ClassDojo's alternative: an ambient teaching assistant that listens, helps, and acts without the teacher having to do anything different. "We can give every teacher a teaching assistant. That's a much more humane interface, where you don't have to do anything."

And Brent again, from the counselor's perspective. School counselors were spending their time on compliance paperwork for career plans. The compliance was being met. The advising was not happening. Oklahoma's field team model didn't eliminate the paperwork. It put a real person in the building who could help counselors shift from box-checking to genuine student conversations.

The pattern: the prior approach wasn't failing because it was bad technology. It was failing because it required active effort from people who were already overloaded. The new approaches either eliminate the effort entirely (VisPick, ambient AI) or add human support that makes the effort worthwhile (field teams).

### 3. "Show me it actually changed someone's outcome, not just their engagement"

This was the most frequent pattern, four of five panels. And it was the most emotionally charged.

Sam Hyams made the sharpest version of the argument: "Measurement is the bit that's always been missing from the career navigation world. If you think about all the tools that you might use in K-12, college, whatever space you're in, how do you know if they're actually working?" SpringPod's answer is tracking where students end up on LinkedIn after completing programs. For 30,000 students in the Amazon program, a third ended up in tech-aligned roles. That's a destination metric, not a satisfaction survey.

Sam Lam at ClassDojo reported that kids using Sparky, their AI reading tutor, are going "from pre-literacy, so not knowing how to read, to maybe second or third grade level of reading proficiency, in about five months, in 15 minutes a day." That's a grade-level outcome, individually measured.

Josh described the Walmart version: a task that used to take 60 minutes now takes 20. Simple. Measurable. Visible to everyone on the floor.

Reed Hastings connected it to the science. He's funding a study to establish the actual rate of learning under full-time individualized tutoring, referencing Bloom's two-sigma problem: students with individual tutors learn at twice the rate. He also pointed to chess.com, where the typical 10-year-old today scores higher than 10-year-olds 20 years ago on a stable, vertically scored system. "That's the proof of concept. Individualized AI practice, measured outcomes, working at scale."

But Hyams also named the anxiety that runs through all of this: "AI will make it feel better and it will feel like magic, but actually, is it impacting that destination?" That question haunts every case. The worry isn't that the new tools don't work. It's that they produce engagement without evidence of real outcome change, repeating the same pattern that made everyone frustrated with EdTech in the first place.

## Six signals worth watching

Beyond the three recurring jobs, several specific observations from the panels deserve attention. These aren't fully validated patterns. They're signals that showed up with enough behavioral specificity to be worth tracking.

### Trust is built through relationships, not platform UI

The most vivid moment across all five panels came from Brent Haken describing what actually makes technology adoption work in schools: "The only time it really works is when they have somebody that they already trust and they can really rely on to have their cell phone number and call them and cry over the phone when they can't figure it out."

That's not a UX problem. It's a relationship problem. EdTech products that rely on product-led growth within institutions may be structurally underinvesting in the human layer that makes adoption stick.

### One wrong AI answer triggers full abandonment

Josh observed that Walmart associates who get a wrong answer from an AI tool often don't iterate. They abandon the tool entirely. "You get a wrong answer, and I mean, a lot of people are just going to discount it... I give up."

This has implications for any AI tool deployed in frontline or classroom contexts. Products with deterministic or visual outputs (VisPick's green-check confirmation) face lower abandonment risk than probabilistic text generation. Where text generation is necessary, visible confidence signals and easy correction loops matter more than accuracy percentages.

### Institutions suppress their own tools' best features

Reed Hastings told a story from his time at DreamBox. School districts asked him to turn off the adaptive pacing feature, the product's core differentiator, because students who moved ahead of grade level became "disruptive and bored" in regular classes. The institution bought the tool for its adaptive capabilities and then instructed the vendor to disable them.

This is not a solvable sales problem. It's a structural constraint of how classrooms work. EdTech products with adaptive features should explicitly test whether buyers will suppress those features after deployment. Feature adoption audits at 30, 60, and 90 days would catch this.

### AI capability doubling annually resets prior judgments

Hastings argued that AI capabilities are improving so rapidly that products judged "not ready" 18 months ago may now be viable without any changes to their strategy. The doubling curve doesn't just improve existing tools. It dissolves prior objections. Builders and investors should periodically re-evaluate opportunities they previously passed on.

### Screen time anxiety is a design constraint, not a perception problem

ClassDojo's decision to cap Sparky at 15 minutes per day isn't conservative marketing. It's a design response to real parent anxiety about screen time. Sam Lam treated it as a hard constraint, not a preference to overcome. Other products targeting K-5 or family contexts should treat 15 minutes per day as a revealed-preference ceiling and design engagement models that deliver value within it.

### Young people are the most AI-native users

Sam Hyams framed young people as the cohort most naturally comfortable with AI tools, but noted they lack "context on business processes." This suggests a specific product angle: AI that gives young people real business process exposure could deliver employer ROI more directly than traditional work-experience programs. Currently a hypothesis, not a validated pattern. Worth testing.

## What's pushing, what's pulling

Across all five panels, the forces driving these switches have a consistent shape.

**What's pushing people to move:** not incremental dissatisfaction, but structural ceilings. Airbus couldn't serve more than 100 students in person. Oklahoma's mandate wasn't being implemented despite 13 years of trying. Charters produced gains but couldn't scale fast enough. In each case, the prior model didn't just underperform. It hit a wall that no amount of optimization could fix. That's when people start looking for something fundamentally different.

**What's pulling them toward the new:** not technology alone, but technology combined with a human relationship layer. Brent's field team. ClassDojo's ambient assistant that removes the need for teacher effort. Walmart's VisPick that converts scanning time into customer time. The pull is strongest when the new model doesn't just add a tool but changes how people spend their time and attention.

**What almost stops them:** the fear that the new approach produces engagement without outcomes. Sam Hyams voiced it most directly, but it runs under every case. After years of EdTech promising transformation and delivering dashboards, the default assumption is that the new thing will feel better but not actually change results. Proving individual-level outcomes early is the only way past this.

**What keeps them stuck in the old way:** the habit of deploying a platform and expecting behavior change to follow. Brent: "We went through a long time where we were told technology will take care of that for you." Josh: "You don't become a leader by watching a video. You're not gonna become AI proficient by doing a micro certification. This has to be embedded in your jobs." Both describe institutions defaulting to technology deployment without the change management, embedded practice, or human support required for genuine adoption.

## What we don't know yet

This analysis is built from conference panels. That limits what it can tell us. Five specific gaps stand out, each of which would substantially change the picture if filled.

**No student voice.** All five panels are narrated by institutional leaders, vendors, or philanthropists. The learners whose lives are supposedly being transformed are completely absent. We have no data on what students experience when switching from traditional instruction to AI tutoring, from compliance-mode career planning to genuine advising, from classroom learning to individualized practice.

**No frontline practitioner voice.** Teachers, counselors, and Walmart associates are the switching units in three of five cases, but they're all described by their managers or product vendors, not by themselves. What does it actually feel like to be the counselor who used to check boxes and now has a field team member visiting regularly? We're guessing.

**No failed switches.** All five cases are success stories. We have zero data on organizations that tried these new models and reverted to their prior approach. That survivorship bias means the anxiety and habit forces are almost certainly underrepresented.

**No timeline data.** When did dissatisfaction start? How long did people wait before actively looking for alternatives? What was the trigger that moved them from "this isn't great" to "we need to change"? Panel format compresses all of this. The switching timeline is essentially unrecoverable from the current evidence.

**Anxiety forces are thin.** Privacy concerns about ambient AI listening in classrooms, job security fears among frontline workers adopting AI tools, educators worried about losing professional identity as AI handles more teaching, none of these were surfaced in the panels. They almost certainly exist. We just don't have evidence for them yet.

## So what

Five implications for anyone building or buying EdTech tools right now.

**Build the human-technology bundle, not just the technology.** The strongest signal in this entire analysis is Brent's field team model. Not a better platform. A human relationship that makes the platform actually used. If you're building an EdTech product, ask yourself: who is the field team equivalent for your product, and how does your business model make them viable?

**Make outcomes measurable at the individual level before claiming product-market fit.** The measurement gap is named in four of five panels. Products that track destination outcomes, not engagement metrics, will win institutional trust and renewal cycles. If your product can't connect usage to a specific individual-level behavioral change (reading grade level, job placement, task time), build that tracking before you scale.

**Design for zero active effort at first use.** The chatbot model fails because it requires active teacher effort to get value. VisPick succeeds because you hold up your phone and get a green check. ClassDojo's ambient assistant succeeds because "you don't have to do anything." Every additional action required from an overloaded teacher, counselor, or frontline worker is a drop-off point.

**If you're building AI tutoring, start with homeschoolers.** Hastings was explicit: "If you want to change the world, focus on the homeschoolers." Homeschooling families have no institutional grammar to defend, no curriculum coherence constraint, and actively seek adaptive, self-paced tools. They'll generate the behavioral outcome data that institutional buyers will require before they'll consider adoption.

**Test for feature suppression after deployment.** The DreamBox story is a warning. The feature that maximizes learning outcomes may be the feature that institutional buyers disable post-deployment. Run feature adoption audits at 30, 60, and 90 days. If your adaptive or personalized features are being turned off by administrators, that's a leading indicator of churn, not a configuration preference.

## Methodology

This analysis uses a switching-behavior lens adapted from the Jobs-to-be-Done framework. Each panel transcript was analyzed for the forces that drove a change in behavior: what pushed people away from the old approach, what pulled them toward the new one, what anxieties almost stopped the switch, and what habits kept them in the old way. The structured data behind this document is available in the repository's [.jtbd/](../.jtbd/) directory for anyone who wants to examine or extend the analysis.
